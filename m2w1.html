Month 2 - Week 1 (Simplified Cybersecurity Notes)

1. Introduction to Key Legislation and Regulations, Compliance Requirements and Standards

Introduction to Key Legislation and Regulations

Core Areas Covered:

  • • Overview of legal frameworks → Rules that guide cybersecurity practices.
  • • Ethical considerations → Thinking about right vs wrong in cybersecurity.
  • • Professional issues → Responsibilities of professionals in the field.

Definition of Legal Frameworks in Cybersecurity:

  • • Establish rules and regulations to protect digital data, ensure privacy, and maintain trust.
  • • Provide guidelines for handling sensitive data, ensuring compliance, and reducing cyber threats.
  • • Balance individual rights (privacy) with organizational responsibilities (protecting data).

Key Areas of Legal Frameworks:

  • • Data protection → GDPR, CCPA
  • • Industry-specific regulations → HIPAA (healthcare), PCI DSS (payment cards)
  • • Intellectual property rights → Protect ownership of ideas, software, inventions.

Importance and Impact:

  • • Ensure proper security controls and ethical standards.
  • • Define legal liabilities and penalties for non-compliance.
  • • Case Study: WannaCry 2017 ransomware attack showed why GDPR compliance, breach notifications, fines, and stronger protections are needed.
  • • Legislations like GDPR, HIPAA, and CCPA set standards for protecting sensitive information and enforcing accountability.
  • • Relevant across all cybersecurity careers, since they guide risk identification, assessment, and management.

Compliance Requirements and Standards

Definition of Security Standards:

  • • Mandatory controls guiding organizational security initiatives.
  • • Based on policies to stop unauthorized access, theft, and cyber threats.
  • • Globally used to ensure compliance with legal/regulatory requirements.

Creators of Cybersecurity Standards:

  • • Industry experts, professional bodies, and governmental institutes.

Examples of Organizations that Create Standards:

  • • ISO/IEC → Established 1947. International standards for security, safety, and reliability. Notable: ISO/IEC 27000 (security), ISO 9000 (quality).
  • • NIST → Established 1901. US body that develops frameworks like NIST CSF (mandatory for US federal agencies).
  • • PCI SSC → Established 2006 by Visa, Mastercard, Amex, Discover, JCB. Protects card data with PCI DSS.

General Components of Cybersecurity Standards:

  • • Technical controls, organizational controls, compliance guidelines, physical measures.

Detailed Components:

  • • Technical: MFA, encryption, firewalls, monitoring, patching.
  • • Organizational: Policies, risk management, employee training, incident response, vendor compliance.
  • • Compliance: Understand laws (GDPR, HIPAA, PCI DSS), audits, documentation, incident reporting.
  • • Physical: Access control (biometrics/cards), CCTV, locks/fences, fire suppression, power backup.

Examples of Cybersecurity Standards:

  • • ISO/IEC 27001 (ISMS): Global best practice for information security. Risk management approach to protect CIA (Confidentiality, Integrity, Availability). Versions: 2005, 2013, 2022.
  • • PCI DSS: Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard. Created 2006, protects cardholder data. 6 core goals, 12 requirements, mandatory for card-handling businesses.

2. Cybersecurity Frameworks, CSF Core Functions and Cybersecurity Legislations

  • Cybersecurity Frameworks: Guidelines and best practices to manage risks. Usually voluntary, but revised over time.
  • Creators: Industry organizations, government bodies, associations.

Examples of Framework Creators:

  • • NIST → NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF), Risk Management Framework (RMF).
  • • CISA → US agency, collaborates with NIST, focuses on infrastructure protection.
  • • AICPA → Developed SOC 2 (auditing/compliance framework).
  • • ISACA → Created COBIT (governance framework).
  • • CSA → Cloud Controls Matrix (CCM).
  • • CIS → Developed CIS Critical Controls, CIS Benchmarks.

Examples of Frameworks:

  • • NIST CSF 2.0, RMF, SOC 2, ENISA frameworks, COBIT, Cloud Controls Matrix.

NIST CSF Core Functions:

  • • Govern (GV) → Governance, risk strategy, policies.
  • • Identify (ID) → Assets, risks, improvements.
  • • Protect (PR) → Safeguards (access, training, data security).
  • • Detect (DE) → Monitoring, analysis of incidents.
  • • Respond (RS) → Incident management, communication, mitigation.
  • • Recover (RC) → Service restoration, recovery communication.
  • Cybersecurity Legislations: Laws protecting data. Examples: FTC Act (US), Nigerian Cybercrimes Act 2015, EU Cyber Resilience Act.
  • Regulations: Detailed enforceable rules like HIPAA, GDPR.
  • Key Principles: Consent, privacy, security, accountability, transparency, purpose limitation, fairness, control, protection against harm.
  • GDPR (EU Law): Strongest global regulation. Data subject rights include: informed, access, rectify, object, erasure, portability, restrict processing.

3. Introduction to Ethical Hacking and Penetration Testing, Key Ethical Considerations

  • Ethical Hacking: Authorized experts (white hats) hired to test systems legally. Find vulnerabilities before attackers.
  • Types of Hackers: White hat, black hat, gray hat, script kiddies, hacktivists.
  • Penetration Testing: Structured, authorized attempts to exploit vulnerabilities. Types: Black box, white box, gray box.

Key Ethical Considerations:

  • • Authorization → Written permission required.
  • • Transparency → Full communication during testing.
  • • Privacy → Protect sensitive information.
  • • Non-disruption → Avoid outages or harm.
  • • Integrity → Report honestly.
  • • Compliance → Follow laws (GDPR, HIPAA).
  • • Reporting → Clear, unbiased reports and recommendations.

4. Privacy and Data Protection Ethics

  • Principles: Handle data responsibly, access only what's necessary, respect privacy boundaries.
  • Responsible Disclosure: Hackers report privately before public disclosure, often under NDA.
  • Legal Compliance: Follow GDPR, HIPAA, CCPA, etc. Non-compliance leads to penalties.

5. Cybersecurity Policy Development

  • Policy = Document outlining rules to protect confidentiality, integrity, availability of data.
  • Purpose = Define what to protect, threats, and rules.
  • Benefits = Clear standards, regulatory compliance.
  • Challenges = Awareness gaps, balance between security & usability, monitoring & enforcement.

6. Roles of Professional Organizations

  • They create standards, promote best practices, provide certifications, training, and advocacy.
  • Examples: ISC2 (CISSP, CCSP), ISACA (CISA, CISM, CGEIT, CRISC), SANS (GIAC certifications).

7. Public Awareness and Advocacy in Cybersecurity

  • Public Awareness = Education campaigns. Examples: Cybersecurity Awareness Month, Stop. Think. Connect.
  • Advocacy = Influence policy makers, promote stronger laws, encourage compliance.
